Problem Statement

— why this was built

Traditional analog guitar stompboxes offer exceptional sonic warmth but lack flexibility, while software-based multi-effects introduce audible processing latency.

Guitarists seeking complex multi-stage effect chains are often forced to choose between the unwieldy footprint of a physical analog pedalboard or the performance-degrading buffer delays of general-purpose computer or MCU operating systems. High-quality real-time audio requires rigid deterministic tracking to avoid phase alignment problems and jitter.

This project implements custom, hand-coded hardware architectures that ingest raw audio data streams and execute DSP mathematical calculations on a predictable clock cycle, matching analog speeds without compromising digital reconfigurability.

Overview

— scope and approach

This modular audio architecture leverages an FPGA-based processing paradigm to execute synchronous effect pipelines. Guitar pickups are pre-amplified, shifted with a 1.65 V DC bias, and digitized using an external Pmod ADC via the I²S protocol.

Once the parallel data arrives inside the FPGA fabric, modular components execute arithmetic operations sequentially using fixed-point math routines. The finished calculations are fed straight back into a matching I²S DAC, stripped of their DC offsets, and low-pass filtered to clean out radio frequencies and clock artifacts before being delivered to an amplifier at line-level standard.

Architecture

— signal path / system design

The real-time processing pipeline processes continuous data linearly from ingestion to audio playback, utilizing custom internal handshakes for low-overhead interconnectivity.

Implementation

— subsystems and decisions

I²S Transceiver In i2s_in.v

Manages clock serialization and framing alignments to ingest audio from external hardware codecs. Converts serial bits to structured parallel registers sample-accurately.

Hard Clipping DSP hard_clip.v

Implements digital distortion by applying explicit mathematical ceiling and floor boundaries to the signed audio vector, emulating traditional diode distortion square waves.

Delay Line delay.v

Uses configurable internal delay lines to read past audio snapshots out of storage arrays. Provides real-time echo mixing by tracking accurate sample read and write intervals.

FIFO Buffer fifo_buffer.v

Acts as an asynchronous data queue bridging clock phase boundaries safely. Eliminates dropouts or framing errors during cross-domain stream handshakes.

Design Challenges

— tradeoffs and constraints
Challenge 01

Fixed-Point Arithmetic Overflows

Executing mathematical audio filters can easily result in arithmetic register overflows that corrupt the sound. Resolution required custom bit-width extensions and clipping safety checks to handle values within limits without causing register roll-overs.

Challenge 02

Asynchronous Phase Jitter

Ingesting streaming peripheral input clocks alongside internal master frequencies causes timing conflicts. The resolution involved implementing ring FIFO buffers to cleanly re-align frame crossings across clock domain lines safely.
